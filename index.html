About Me

I am a first-year PhD student in the Campbell Research Group at the Autonomous Systems Lab (ASL), fortunate to work under the guidance of Prof. Mark Campbell (John A. Mellowes '60 Professor in Mechanical Engineering). My research focuses on advancing estimation and perception systems for autonomous robots, particularly autonomous vehicles. Currently, I am developing multi-object and multi-target tracking algorithms by integrating probabilistic physics-based models, such as Random Finite Sets (RFS), with deep learning techniques for semantic segmentation and spatial reasoning.

I am interested in developing innovative solutions that enhance the intelligence, efficiency, and reliability of autonomous systems.I believe that the immense potential of robotics and autonomous systems remains largely untapped, awaiting exploration and application worldwide. In the upcoming years, progress in this domain will significantly influence not only our industries and economic landscapes but also our daily lives and consequently, the broader society. I aspire to play a crucial role in driving the advancement of robotic systems. That's why I find fulfillment in addressing some of the most complex and intricate challenges within this field throughout my research endeavors.

Academic Projects

For me, completing projects is one of the most interactive & effective ways to implement the knowledge that I learn in classroom into real-world systems. Below you will find some of my completed projects that have provided me valuable knowledge & exposure to the challenges faced when applying engineering concepts in real-world.

QuadSafe: MPC for Quadrotor with Safety Barriers and Stability

The project explores the combination of Model Predictive Control (MPC) with Control Barrier Functions (CBFs) and Control Lyapunov Functions (CLFs) to improve both safety and stability in autonomous quadrotors. By integrating MPC’s predictive capabilities with the real-time safety guarantees offered by CBFs and the stability assurances provided by CLFs, the proposed framework delivers robust control for the highly nonlinear dynamic system, enabling it to reach target destinations while avoiding both static and dynamic obstacles.

Personalized Constraint Learning for Autonomous Vehicle Control Using Privileged Information

Autonomous car users have varying risk and safety preferences, making it challenging to translate them into explicit policy constraints. This project leverages privileged learning and knowledge distillation, using an MPC-CBF expert to provide demonstrations for training a student policy via DAgger. The student network learns to map user preference labels to constraints on physical quantities like velocities and safe distances, ensuring safer, more intuitive, and responsive control.

Localization, Mapping, and Motion Planning for Autonomous Mobile Robots

Developed an autonomous mobile robot. Implemented Particle Filter for robot localization by fusing measurement data from a depth sensor and a beacon sensor. Designed a geometric approach to update the map recursively based on measurements from the depth sensor. Implemented Probabilistic Roadmaps and Dijkstra's Algorithm for navigating in the environment while traveling to desired locations/waypoints.

Comparative Analysis of Localization Filters for Quadrotors

Implemented multiple filters to achieve full-state (position and orientation) estimation of a Quadrotor. Used the nonlinear dynamics of a quadrotor in the Extended Kalman Filter, Sigma Point Filter (Unscented Kalman Filter), Information Filter, Particle Filter, Extended Kalman Filter with smoother, and Extended Kalman Filter with measurement gating for localizing the quadrotor. Compared the performance of these filters and simulated the drone in MATLAB.

Automated Writing: Scripting using Robot Manipulator

The goal of the project was to teach a robotic manipulator (Widowx 250s) with 6 degree-of-freedom to replicate the alphanumeric characters written by a human. Four control strategies were implemented to achieve this objective: Dynamic Movement Primitives (DMPs), Recurrent Neural Network (RNN), Model Predictive Control (MPC), and Simple Waypoint Tracking. We also performed a comparative evaluation of each of these techniques for a given set of characters.

RpiPiano: A Capacitive Touch Mini-piano

Designed a Capacitive Touch Keyboard. This was implemented by connecting capacitive touch sensors to the GPIO ports of the Raspberry Pi 4. When a key is pressed, a speaker connected to the RPi outputs the corresponding note. To add further controls to the keyboard, we designed a user interface on the piTFT screen that allows the user to change the keyboard octave, play a metronome in the background, and switch to “Drum Mode,” where all the keys are mapped to drum sound effects rather than piano keys.

Vehicle Steering using Model Predictive Control

Designed and implemented a Model Predictive Controller (MPC) considering the physical constraints for steering the vehicle. This MPC facilitated lane changes for automobiles while averting oncoming obstacles. Additionally, developed a mathematical model to analyze disturbances caused by strong crosswinds alongside the vehicle. This model enhanced comprehension of the controller's performance in real-world conditions. The project was chosen as one of the class's best projects.

Modeling and simulation of quick return motion mechanism

Developed an analytical model for the quick return motion mechanism, a modified four-bar chain that enables the end effector to move faster in one direction than the other. This model was developed using the core principles of Mechanics. The equations of motion for the four-bar mechanism were derived employing the Euler-Lagrange Method and Differential-Algebraic System of Equations Approach. The system was simulated in MATLAB under various conditions using the derived equations.

Color sorting robotic manipulator

Devised and assembled a three-degree-of-freedom robotic manipulator from scratch, employing DC servo motors at the joints to actuate the links. Additionally, a gripper was crafted using a gear mechanism to grasp objects and accurately position them according to their respective colors. The gripper featured a mounted color sensor for this purpose. Control of the motors and decision-making based on measurements from the color sensor were managed by a standard Arduino microcontroller.

Smartphone Controlled Peaucellier Mechanism

Constructed a physical model of the Peaucellier Mechanism, a planar linkage used for converting rotary motion precisely into straight-line motion. Employed a DC servo motor to regulate the driving link by rotating it along an arc of a circle. Control over the motor was facilitated by an Arduino Uno, linked to a Bluetooth module. This configuration allowed us to command the mechanism via smartphone directives, including on/off functionalities, rotation speed adjustments, and reversing the direction of rotation.

Analysis of ATV Powertrain for SAE BAJA

Conducted design assessments to validate the compatibility of the internal combustion engine and its connected gearbox for the SAE BAJA all-terrain vehicle (ATV). These evaluations were performed following industry-standard protocols for each component within the engine and gearbox assembly. Using these calculations, a CAD model was developed of the engine and gearbox assembly in Autodesk Inventor. Subsequently, simulation were performed using this CAD model in ANSYS Workbench to evaluate the powertrain components, ensuring their appropriateness.

Finite Element Analysis of a classroom bench

Developed a true-to-scale CAD model of a classroom bench in Autodesk Inventor, meticulously incorporating structural elements such as steel bars and wooden planks to ensure accurate representation. Employed ANSYS Workbench for simulations, utilizing Finite Element techniques. These simulations were aimed at exploring and understanding the potential failure points or criteria of the bench when subjected to a diverse range of loading conditions-both static and dynamic in nature.

Target detection and tracking using multiple pinhole cameras

Implemented the sensor model of a pinhole pan and tilt camera in MATLAB for detecting and tracking different stationary and dynamic targets (rigid bodies) in a given workspace. The algorithm, considering the sensor's physical limitations, enables real-time tracking of multiple targets by dispersing them across multiple cameras. Moreover, a pre-trained Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) was employed to classify the detected targets throughout the simulation.

Hydraulic Press System

Built a hydraulic press system model utilizing PVC pipes and pressure gauges to illustrate Pascal's Law in fluid mechanics and the Hydrostatic Paradox. This system involves two rams positioned in distinct pipes filled with fluid, typically water, interconnected to each other. The alteration in pressure within one pipe, induced by the force on the ram, is transmitted uniformly and without reduction to the other pipe (and ram). The pressure gauge measurements further demonstrate the Hydrostatic Paradox.
